Non-Profit Website Redesign

Redesigning a local non-profits website ensuring responsiveness between desktop and mobile.

Overview

As a local non profit with large scale ambition, Fill a Heart 4 Kids relies heavily on volunteers & monetary donations to sustain their outreach programs. Currently, their mobile and desktop site lacks the necessary UI to attract and maintain new contributors.

My Role

UX Designer & Researcher

(Testing, Synthesis, Ideation, Prototyping, Graphic Design)

Team

Sara Bell & Kenny Kravitz

Project Type

Conceptual

Timeline

3 Weeks

Tools

Figma, Figjam, Trello, Google Docs & Sheets

Solution

Curate a well organized, responsive web design system to optimize user experience leading to continued engagement and recurring donations.

User Research

Objectives

Before we could implement any solutions as designers, as researchers we needed to understand:

How users choose a non-profit to send monetary donations to?

Why users continue to engage with a certain organization?

Once these objectives were established we formulated a series of questions with the goal of gaining insights from potential users of sites like Fill A Heart 4 Kids.

As a team we collectively completed:

1 stakeholder interview

6 User Interviews

1 Independent survey with 10 responses

Findings

Once we compared the responses from the interviews and survey through mapping out an affinity diagram, we discovered that due to negative experiences in the past each of our prospective users needed a demonstration of trustworthiness from their ideal organizations to secure their donation.

Affinity Diagram

Users believed that their organizations were deemed trustworthy when they were able to effectively portray authenticity, transparency, and convenience through their online presence and donation portals.

Users noted primary ways that an organization could demonstrate these characteristics to gain their trust:

Authenticity

  • Creating a personal/ emotional connection through strong imagery and data
  • Personable communication and connection post donation

Transparancy

  • Clear representation and definition of the organizations mission and goals
  • Seeing direct impact of monetary donations

Convenience

  • Quick informed donation transactions
  • Ability to choose how and when to donate
  • Familiar and trusted payment platforms

Definition & Synthesis

Utilizing data from our user research we defined a user statement that would allow us to keep the primary user needs in mind throughout the design process:

User Statement

User is a kind-hearted person who is seeking to build a connection with a smaller nonprofit organization because they want to continuously donate to a trustworthy cause, but some organizations they've come across lack transparency and authenticity, often feeling more transactional than emotional.

We then created a user persona based on research findings to help us build empathy for the user, fully understanding their goals, motivations, needs, and pain points.

Defining Our Goal

How might we create a more authentic and transparent experience for users looking for a trustworthy nonprofit to engage with, so that they feel compelled to continue supporting longterm by sending donations, while experiencing a more personal donating experience?

Once we were equipped with the informed understanding of our user's needs that would ensure their engagement and donations, we moved on to analyzing and testing the current site by conducting a heuristic evaluation. We did this to discover what was currently aligning with our user research and what might be missing.

We liked the positive and warm voice the site was going for, but ran into many pain points that turned what should have been a positive experience, negative.

Heuristic Evaluation

Janet's needs aren't currently being met by FAH4K based on information obtained from an heuristic evaluation

The site currently offers:

  • Positive and Warm voice
  • Cramped, text & illustration heavy sections
  • Overwhelming amount of content per section
  • Confusing navigation and naming conventions
  • Colors that fail contrast standards
  • Hard to consume font choices.

We found that despite the serious nature of their mission, the site lacked a sense of balance between a tone of playfulness and maturity that would compel someone to connect and donate to the cause.

Ideation

To identify pain points in the information architecture and navigation of the site, we mapped out a user flow in which our user “Janet” recently heard about Fill a Heart 4 Kids, and wants to learn more about their organization by exploring their website and possibly make a donation.

User Insights

When conducting usability testing based on our user flow, we found key insights and pain points that users ran into when attempting to complete tasks. Some key insights being that users commonly felt overwhelmed by the information on the site, and also confused whether they were navigating to the right page.

Usability testing insights:

  • Overwhelmed by how information was displayed
  • Little spacing between sections made information hard to consume
  • Navigation caused users to be unsure where the website was leading them
  • Confused by unconventional format of clickable items
  • Felt they spent more time than they should have to complete a task
  • Hierarchy of information seemed out of order

Ideas

Findings from User interviews informed our ideation process. Based on our research we came up with a focused set of ideas for our site redesign.

Feature Prioritization using the “I Like, I Wish, What if” method:

  • I wish I could see testimonials from people who are impacted or involved
  • I like seeing images & videos that are authentic relate to impact
  • I like seeing the mission at the forefront of the site
  • I wish I knew more about the founders & their story
  • I like seeing up to date statistics & data
  • I like seeing examples of care packages I could donate

We then utilized a feature prioritization matrix method to map out commonalities and determine importance.

To inform our redesign of the information architecture of the site, card sorting was conducted to group together information in a way that is commonly understood by users. Common groupings of information would become pages and common titles of these groups would become primary navigation headers.

Prototyping

Low-fidelity

We created a paper prototype based on the findings from our previous research. We started off with a mobile design to ensure responsiveness transitioning to desktop.

Solutions

Users noted that current mobile navigation didn't meet standards leaving users confused.

Create a more intuitive & user friendly navigation system:

  • Header navigation now sticks to the top of the page while scrolling
  • Opening the navigation keeps you connected to the same page without feeling lost
  • New secondary page titles based on data collected from user testing

Create a more digestible and visually appealing layout and information architecture:

  • Hierarchy of information on site based on user needs from testing
  • Visual lightness by showcasing powerful imagery rather than heavy text

User Testing

Usability testing was conducted to provide us with key user insights and help discover pain points in the architecture of our design.

Tasks for user testing focused on learning more about the organization and donating to a specific cause. We focused on these tasks since they are the main goals and function of the site.

Task 1

Where would you find out more information regarding the nonprofit itself?

Assumption: User will see the mission statement clearly outlined when landing on the home Page but want to know more and will navigate the hamburger menu to find the “about us” tab.

Task 2

After learning more about the organization you decide you want to donate a care package. Where would you navigate to find and select a care package ?

Assumption: User will use hamburger menu and navigate to “How you can help”.

Outcomes & Findings

Some notable changes informed by user insights shown here:

  • On the left, we changed a donate button into a button that links to a primary page of the site
  • We also removed an upcoming events calendar from the home page as it was noted that users felt it was out of place on the homepage
  • On the right we also changed the structure of cards on the “How you can help” page, simplifying the user experience by slimming down the amount of buttons a user sees on the page

Prototyping

Mid-fidelity

User Testing

Outcomes & Findings

Another round of usability testing was conducted on our mid-fidelity prototype. Some notable changes informed by user insights are shown here:

  • We noticed that users were confused when all call to action buttons looked the same. In order to differentiate page buttons and allow the donate button to stand out more, we filled it in with pink and added a secondary button style, making the others an outline.
  • We learned from testing that users wanted a personalized experience, so adjusting the copy to be more personal was important. Button wording and section rewordings: “Donate” changed to “give now” “Get involved” changed to “together, we can make change”.
  • It was noted that users were unsure what page the “Make change” button would lead them to. We changed this to a “how you can help” button to connect the button more to the page destination.
  • The hierarchy of infromation was also changed based on user testing. On the "About Us" page, “Our Team” was moved to the bottom. Allowing users to see "Our Impact" followed by testimonials after reading the "Our Mission" statement.

Style Tile

Since users were overwhelmed by the text we adjusted the display and main font. We chose Calistoga as our display font; a cheerful serif typeface that matches the tone of the organization and having more weight making it easier for users to read. It also allowed us to develop a very clear hierarchy.

We also rebranded the Logo utilizing this change in typeface and changed the logo structure to ensured readability.

We wanted a similar color palette to what is already being used by the organization. We adjusted the tones to ensure they pass color accessibility testing. The current pink color that is used throughout the website, did not pass testing.

To bring in the authenticity that is important to users, we decided to remove the illustrations and use only real imagery we sourced from the organizations social media.

Conclusion

Final Prototype

During initial testing, users shared that the amount of text on the pages were difficult to consume.

We resolved this by concealing some of the text behind images, allowing the user to choose when to interact with the content while using the imagery and titles to engage the user in a more visually compelling way.

I created a donation overly that allows users to easily access information on what they could donate towards. I added this to ensure users had access to information about where their money is going towards when they decide its time to donate

Future Opportunities

If we had more time on this case study we would have built out the “Get Involved” page featuring ways to volunteer or host events.

We also would have built out a more customizable donation platform that allowed users to choose how they want to donate money - tying in the convenience aspect discovered in user interviewers.
