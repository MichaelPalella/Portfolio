Government Agency Website Redesign

Redesigning a government agency website ensuring responsiveness between desktop and mobile.

Overview

Energy.gov could be a great resource for Americans to learn about and take advantage of clean energy incentives. A redesign is needed focusing on improving Navigation and Information Architecture to reduce uncertainty when navigating, and improve overall user experience.

My Role

UX Designer & Researcher

(Testing, Synthesis, Ideation, Prototyping, Graphic Design)

Project Type

Conceptual

Timeline

5 Weeks

Tools

Figma, Figjam, Trello, Google Docs & Sheets

Solution

Curate a responsive web design with a complete overhaul of the Information Architecture, leading to users successfully navigating to sources that are most important for them.

Design Process

25% User Research

Why Visit Energy.gov?

Based on my design point of view and analysis of the current site, a user visits energy.gov to access authoritative and comprehensive information on:

  • Energy policies
  • Sustainable practices
  • Renewable energy sources
  • Government incentives
  • Educational resources

They aim to make informed decisions about energy consumption, efficiency, and contribute to a more sustainable and resilient energy future.

I created a Proto Persona based on my site analysis to personify the goals, needs, and pain points of the user.

User Research

Objectives

I wanted to understand how users currently navigate the site. To do this I defined a user path to help compare how the site is currently designed to complete a task, with the way a user attempts to complete a task.

Based on my site analysis a user visits Energy.gov for two primary reasons:

  1. To learn about how to save energy & money
  2. To learn about upgrading to new clean energy

Tasks would relate to these objectives.

Five user tests were conducted. Each user was asked to complete two tasks within a 10 minute time limit.

Task 1

As a user, I want to find tips on saving energy at home and identify three practical changes I can make to reduce my energy consumption and lower my utility bills.

Task 2

As a user, I want to learn about the benefits and considerations of installing residential solar panels. I want to learn about potential cost savings, environmental impact, and available incentives.

Another round of usability tests were conducted with tasks focusing on utilizing navigation elements of the website to help identify common pain points within the navigation structure.

The Usability tests identified common pain points, leading to users not successfully completing the task assigned to them.

Findings

Through testing I discovered that common roadblocks for users were navigation, scannability, and mis-clicking.

Navigation

  • Users were confused, navigating back and forth between pages
  • Spent extensive time scrolling up and down
  • Easy to get lost with so many branching paths
  • Users could not complete tasks, getting lost on their search

Scannability

  • Large blocks of text that's not easily scannable
  • Contained jargon that users were not familiar with
  • Not enough visual aid.
  • Information seemed cramped together

Mis-clicking

  • A lot of clicking to find answer
  • Overwhelming amount of content to click on per page

Common Findings

  • Information is too condensed and overwhelming
  • Topics not split up in an intuitive or logical way
  • Hard to pinpoint where to look for something
  • Too many branching paths
  • Too many options to choose from
  • Extensive scrolling
  • Feel more lost as they scroll through more information

Comments were made about the site not feeling catered to an average user. Users commonly felt overwhelmed when attempting to complete a task.

Defintion & Synthesis

I conducted a heuristic evaluation of the navigation of the homepage and the “Save Energy, Save Money” secondary page to help identify the issues in architecture that are causing user roadblocks.

Why Redesign?

  • It feels almost impossible to find what you are looking for on the site
  • Categories aren't labeled in a meaningful way and aren't where you would expect they would be
  • There is a lack of structure that makes it feel like you are searching for a needle in a haystack

Redesign What? Why?

I will start off be redesigning the main navigation along with the Home and “Save Energy, Save Money" Secondary Page. Based on my site analysis these pages contain information regarding the main reasons people visit Energy.gov.

I want to make sure it's clear for a user to easily navigate to the most important information on the site through the main navigation.

Ideation

In order to inform my redesign of the sitemap, cards were created from the current navigation and topics from the main pages of the site. These were reorganized by users to improve the information architecture of the site to be more user friendly.

Referencing data from the card sorting results and utilizing LATCH principles, I created a new sitemap based on testing that is more intuitive for the user to navigate.

User Testing

5 second tests were conducted to check if my design was on the right path in being intuitive and focused.

For the first set of tests, I wanted to learn what users took away from just a few seconds of looking through the site. I asked the user to describe what they remembered from 5 seconds on the site and what stood out to them.

For the second set of tests, I focused on the redesigned navigation by asking the user to navigate to a specific secondary page.

The second set of user testing was also reassuring with users being able to quickly complete the task.

User Testing

8 user tests were conducted focused on navigating the site to complete tasks and also describing the experience using adjectives.

I wanted to check if tasks were easily completed by the user and identify any pain points. I also wanted to ensure the brand identity and voice described was helpful, innovative, intellectual, and dependable.

Task 1

Use the navigation to find information on how to save money and cut costs at Home

Task 2

Looking at the content on the homepage find information on how to save money and cut costs at Home

Outcomes & Findings

I discovered that users enjoyed the experience and easily completed the tasks. I also found that 4 out of 8 users used the adjective “simple” to describe the site. I wanted to change the feeling of simplicity to intellectual in my next iteration.

Final Thoughts & Reflection

The U.S. Department of energy contains a lot of very useful information, but it was almost impossible for an average user to navigate to find what they were looking for.

The most crucial task I had to focus on was a complete overhaul of the information architecture. Redesigning the sitemap ensured that users could find information where they would expect it to be, creating a more intuitive and enjoyable experience.

When creating a responsive web design that can shift between screen sizes whether on desktop or mobile, its best to start with the mobile design first. “It's easier to move from a small apartment to a big house”.

Future Opportunities

While redesigning I wanted to make sure that my goal of having a brand identity and voice that aligned with being helpful, innovative, intellectual, and dependable were apparent in the look and feel of the site. In the future I would like to iterate on this even more to create a site that has an even more clear brand identity.
